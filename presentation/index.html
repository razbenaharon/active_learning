The metric dictates the strategy

Only the positive class scores. True negatives contribute nothing to F1, so a label is worth buying mainly if it is — or teaches us about — a “Left”.

The 0.5 threshold is welded shut. The grader calls predict(). We cannot tune the threshold, so the only lever on the precision/recall balance is what the forest is trained on.

Those two facts produced our two design choices: hunt positives, then reweight by duplication.

What to buy: hunt positives, don’t refine the boundary

Same 5,000-query budget, three acquisition rules. Textbook uncertainty sampling loses to simply asking for the most-likely positives.

More positives ⇒ higher F1, monotonically. Uncertainty sampling spends the budget near P=0.5, where labels are ambiguous but cheap to be wrong about — it buys 732 fewer positives and loses 0.0155 F1. When the metric is minority-class F1, the classic rule is the wrong rule.

When to buy: 5 rounds beat one big order

Retraining the scout between rounds sharpens its ranking — but each round strips the pool of easy positives, and the yield decays toward the base rate.

The first scout sees only 500 labels, so its ranking is noisy; each round it learns from hundreds more positives and ranks better. That feedback is worth +240 positives for free. But precision falling 79.5% → 44.3% is also the warning: by round 5 we are nearly guessing, which is exactly why more budget would not save us (slide 5).

The threshold is fixed — so we move the data instead

Duplicating each known positive shifts the forest’s vote share toward “Left”. It is the only recall lever we are allowed to pull.

Honest reading: the 1× → 2× jump (+0.0178) is 3½× the noise floor — real. But 2× vs 3× differ by 0.0006, and the wobble at 2.25–2.5× is the same size as the gap. So we claim only what the data supports: duplication matters; the exact ratio in [2, 3] does not. We picked 2× as the flat, robust centre — not because 0.6471 “won”.

Why the best “winner” was rejected

Tiered oversampling gained +0.0010 — one-fifth of the noise floor — and it hurt seed 1. Adopting it would be fitting our own test set, not improving the method.

Two results that surprised us

Query-by-committee ≡ probability. A forest already is a committee — its predict_proba is the tree vote share. Identical ranking, zero gain.

Dropping hard negatives backfired (0.647 → 0.627). Our labeled set is already 60% positive; those boundary negatives are the only thing teaching precision.

Where the ceiling comes from

Round-5 precision is 44%, closing on the 33% base rate. The ~1,800 positives we never catch look like “Stayed” to this forest. Not our algorithm — the features.

What we would tell the next team

Read the metric before writing the algorithm. “F1 of a minority class at a frozen threshold” quietly rewrites the problem from “label the most informative points” to “find the most positives and train on the right class ratio.” Every gain we made came from that sentence; every idea that ignored it lost.

Measure your noise floor first. Ours was ±0.005 — from training row order alone. Without it we would have “improved” the model nine times and shipped nothing but overfitting.
